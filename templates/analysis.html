Weather & Total Accident Counts
Impact of Weather Conditions on Total Accident Counts

The boxplots depict how accident numbers in New York City during 2022 varied across various weather conditions. Rain and cloudy weather show a wide range of accident counts, with rain exhibiting some outliers, indicating occasional spikes in accident numbers during such weather. Clear days have a relatively compact distribution but with a slight skew towards higher accident counts. Snowy conditions, while having fewer accidents overall, display a tight distribution with fewer outliers, suggesting a more consistent accident rate during such conditions. Overall, the variability in accident counts is greatest during rainy and cloudy weather, which could be due to the impact of these conditions on visibility and road surface conditions, affecting driving safety.

Impact of Temperature on Total Accident Counts

The boxplots visualize the distribution of crashes across different temperature levels during 2022 in New York City. Moderate temperatures seem to have a higher median number of crashes compared to other categories, with a notable spread of data points indicating variability in crash frequency. Cold temperatures show a similar range of crash numbers, with outliers suggesting occasional spikes in crashes during colder weather. Freezing temperatures have the smallest interquartile range, indicating more consistent crash numbers, while warm and hot temperatures show a wider spread, with warm temperatures having a slightly higher median number of crashes. This information could suggest that driver behavior or road conditions at moderate to warm temperatures might contribute to higher crash frequencies.

Weather-Related Distributions of Traffic Accidents

These collection of graphs presents a comprehensive view of weather conditions associated with accident data in top 10 areas with the most traffic accidents in 2022.

Contrary to our initial assumption that an increase in accidents is likely during adverse weather conditions, the plots reveal an opposing trend that most accidents occur under moderate weather conditions, with extreme weather being less commonly associated with traffic accidents.

  • The precipitation graph indicates that dry conditions are most common during accidents, with a notable decrease in accident counts as precipitation increases.
  • Temperature-related accidents are fairly distributed across 'Freezing Cold', 'Cold', and 'Moderate' categories, with fewer occurring in 'Warm' and 'Hot' conditions.
  • High humidity seems to be the most prevalent condition in accidents, suggesting a possible impact on road safety.
  • Wind levels mostly result in accidents when flags wave or leaves scatter, which are moderate wind conditions.
  • Snow-related accidents are overwhelmingly more frequent when there is no snow because there were only 18 snowy days in 2022.
  • Visibility is predominantly high during most accidents, indicating that poor visibility is less commonly a contributing factor.

This observation is valid as individuals tend not to go out on days with heavy rain or extreme heat. Notwithstanding with, the plot directs us to model the relative traffic volume between days with favorable and unfavorable weather conditions.

Temporal Analysis

The time series chart illustrates daily crash counts over a period, overlaid with both a daily average (green dashed line) and a 7-day moving average (red dashed line). The blue dots represent daily crash data, with spikes indicating days with higher-than-average crashes. The 7-day moving average smooths out daily fluctuations, providing a clearer view of the overall trend, which helps identify patterns or shifts in accident frequency over time. The daily average appears relatively constant, serving as a benchmark against which daily and weekly variations can be assessed. Peaks above the daily average suggest days with unusually high accident counts, while troughs indicate fewer accidents than average.

Seasonality by Month

The line graph displays the monthly crash count for 2022. The x-axis represents the month of the year, while the y-axis shows the number of crashes. The blue line indicates the actual number of crashes each month, while the red dashed line represents the monthly average crash count for the year.

There's a noticeable fluctuation in crash numbers. Notably, there is a significant peak around the middle of the year, particularly in May and June. The number of crashes remains high until October, coinciding with the onset of colder days when people tend to stay indoors. There is a sharp decline in the number of crashes during holiday seasons, particularly at the beginning and end of the year.

Disaggregated Time Study

The heatmap displays the number of accidents by weekday and hour, providing a detailed temporal breakdown of when accidents occur. Clustering near New York University

Clustering near Columbia University

The two visualizations provided appear to be the outcomes of a K-Means clustering analysis applied to accident data in New York City, with one focusing on the area near Columbia University and the other near New York University. The key variables considered in the clustering process include precipitation levels, the time of accidents, and the severity of accidents. Despite the thoughtful selection of variables, the results seem to show limitations in terms of producing visually distinctive groupings.

This limitation could be due to the inherent complexity and variability of accident data, where significant overlaps in conditions and outcomes are common, making it challenging for clustering algorithms to find clear separations. It's possible that the factors considered, while relevant, are not sufficient to create distinct groupings or that the scale of the map does not allow for easy visual discrimination of clusters.

Despite the challenges in differentiating clusters visually, the maps offer useful observations. For instance, a comparison between the regions surrounding Columbia University (uptown) and New York University (downtown) indicates a disparity in accident occurrence, with Columbia's uptown vicinity experiencing a reduced number of incidents relative to the bustling downtown near NYU. Conversely, the similarity in the color patterns across both areas suggests that the clustering outcomes do not reveal any significant variance between the environments of the two institutions.
