Daily Time Series

The time series chart illustrates daily crash counts over a period, overlaid with both a daily average (green dashed line) and a 7-day moving average (red dashed line). The blue dots represent daily crash data, with spikes indicating days with higher-than-average crashes. The 7-day moving average smooths out daily fluctuations, providing a clearer view of the overall trend over time. We can observe that:

  • There is a weekly seasonality in accidents.
  • Troughs, indicating fewer accidents than average, usually occur in winter and during holiday seasons.
  • Summer is the peak season for accidents.

Seasonality by Month

The line graph displays the monthly crash count for 2022. The x-axis represents the month of the year, while the y-axis shows the number of crashes. The blue line indicates the actual number of crashes each month, while the red dashed line represents the monthly average crash count for the year. We can observe that:

  • There is a monthly seasonality in accidents.
  • there is a significant peak around the middle of the year, particularly in May and June. The number of crashes remains high until October, coinciding with the onset of colder days when people tend to stay indoors.
  • There is a sharp decline in the number of crashes during holiday seasons, particularly at the beginning and end of the year.

Weather & Total Accident Counts

The boxplots depict how accident numbers in New York City during 2022 varied across various weather conditions. We observe that:

  • Rain and cloudy weather show a wide range of accident counts, with rain exhibiting some high outliers.
  • Clear days have a relatively compact distribution but with a slight skew towards higher accident counts.
  • Snowy conditions have fewer accidents overall and display a tight distribution with fewer outliers, likely due to limited samples (as there were only 18 snowy days in 2022) in our dataset.

Overall, the variability in accident counts is greatest during rainy and cloudy weather, possibly due to the impact of these conditions on visibility and road surface conditions, affecting driving safety.


Lets's dive into additional weather details to examine the impact of weather on daily accident counts.




The patterns mostly align with our general expectation that more crashes tend to occur on days with bad weather conditions, partly confirming the validity of our dataset.

  • Wind Level: On days with strong winds, the median number of crashes tend to be higher than the other and we see high outliers of crashes on days with "small tree sway" wind level.
  • Temperature: We have the most day counts for warm and moderate temperatures, with moderate days showing the greatest spread of data points across all temperatures. We observe a high median number of crashes on hot days. Warm days also exhibit a slightly higher median number of crashes.
  • Precipitation: We observe the highest median, 25th quantile, and 75th quantile number of traffic crashes, as well as high outliers, on days with heavy precipitation. Additionally, the median number of traffic crashes on moderate precipitation days is the lowest.
  • Visibility: Most days are of good visibility in 2022. The median number of crashes are on a similar level across all visibility levels. We identify some high outliers and a higher 25th quantile number on days with poor visibility.

Top Contributing Factors
Overall Accident Counts by Contributing Factors

The plot shows a count of accidents sorted by contributing factors, arranged in descending order. In the chart, 'Driver inattention/Distraction' emerges as the most prevalent contributing factor to accidents, closely followed by 'Failure to Yield Right-of-Way'—a characteristic pattern observed in NYC.


Let's dive into the influence of weather conditions on the contributing factors.

We identify that:

  • "Driver Inattention/Distraction" consistently dominates as a leading factor across all conditions and accident types, maintaining its prominence.
  • On snowy days, "Failure to Yield Right-of-Way" is also prevalent, constituting 51.77% of pedestrian-involved accidents and 24.64% of cyclist-involved accidents.
  • On freezing cold days, "Unsafe Speed" is notably more problematic, with a significantly higher relative frequency compared to other seasons.
  • A noteworthy trend emerges when comparing contributing factors between pedestrians and cyclists, with "Bicyclist error" taking a more significant role in causing accidents, surpassing "Pedestrian error" in cases involving non-motorists.

The last set of plots directs us to take a closer look into the impact of additional weather details on accidents involving cyclists, considering some noticeable differences across various weather conditions. In contrast, the plot for pedestrian-involved accidents show few discernible differences. Note that in the rightmost chart, despite variations in contributing factors across different weather conditions for accidents involving both pedestrians and cyclists, the total number of cases is 45, potentially tempering the observable effects shown in this plot.


Other than the already discussed two most prominent contributing factors, we take a closer look at the Bicyclist-specific causes here. Some specific weather conditions appear to amplify the role of "Bicyclist Error" in contributing to accidents involving cyclists. Particularly, "Bicyclist Error/Confusion" becomes more pronounced on:

  • Extremely cold days
  • Days with poor visibility
  • Days with significantly strong wind intensity.

Severity of Accidents
Note:

To simplify the discussion, we classify the accidents based on the total number of injuries and deaths. An accident is labeled as 'Minor' if there are no injuries or deaths, 'Serious' if there are injuries but no deaths, and 'Fatal' if it involves deaths. Within the 'Serious' category, accidents are further divided into subcategories: 'Serious (Low)' with 0-3 injuries, 'Serious (Medium)' with 3-10 injuries, and 'Serious (High)' with more than 10 injuries. In the 'Fatal' category, accidents are additionally segmented into 'Fatal' with 0-3 deaths and 'Very Fatal' with more than 3 deaths.

We can discern subtle distinctions among categories:

  • The occurrence of very fatal cases seems to be higher on days with heavy precipitation and higher temperatures compared to days with no rain and milder temperatures.
  • Within the visibility category, there is a lower frequency of minor cases on days with poor visibility compared to those with clearer visibility.

However, we are unable to pinpoint significant differences across categories, and therefore, there are no clear clues regarding the degree of impact weather has on the severity of traffic accidents.


Relative Frequency of Serious Accidents in Non-Motorists-Involved Cases

Let's categorize accidents based on the parties involved and delve into each category more closely. The defined categories include:

  • Only Pedestrians: Accidents involving pedestrians victims exclusively.
  • Only Cyclists: Accidents involving cyclists victims exclusively.
  • Pedestrians & Motorists: Accidents involving both pedestrians and motorists victims.
  • Pedestrians & Cyclists: Accidents involving both pedestrians and cyclists victims.
  • Cyclists & Motorists: Accidents involving both cyclists and motorists victims.

Accidents exclusively involving motorists victims are excluded from the analysis due to their high frequency, which could overshadow the presence of the other two types of cases.

Note that we define involvement by whether the party sustains injuries and/or fatalities in the accident. Specifically, our analysis focuses on accidents with a severity level beyond minor, meaning there are injuries and/or fatalities.

These adjacent plots display accident counts and the relative frequency of accidents at various severity levels involving nonmotorists. We observe that:

  • Pedestrians are more susceptible to injuries in accidents, with the majority involving fewer than 3 injuries.
  • As the number of parties involved in an accident increases, the severity level tends to rise. Notably, the two accidents involving pedestrians, cyclists, and motorists resulted in fatalities.

Clustering near New York University

Clustering near Columbia University

The two visualizations provided appear to be the outcomes of a K-Means clustering analysis applied to accident data in New York City, with one focusing on the area near Columbia University and the other near New York University. The key variables considered in the clustering process include precipitation levels, the time of accidents, and the severity of accidents. Despite the thoughtful selection of variables, the results seem to show limitations in terms of producing visually distinctive groupings.

This limitation could be due to the inherent complexity and variability of accident data, where significant overlaps in conditions and outcomes are common, making it challenging for clustering algorithms to find clear separations. It's possible that the factors considered, while relevant, are not sufficient to create distinct groupings or that the scale of the map does not allow for easy visual discrimination of clusters.

Despite the challenges in differentiating clusters visually, the maps offer useful observations. For instance, a comparison between the regions surrounding Columbia University (uptown) and New York University (downtown) indicates a disparity in accident occurrence, with Columbia's uptown vicinity experiencing a reduced number of incidents relative to the bustling downtown near NYU. Conversely, the similarity in the color patterns across both areas suggests that the clustering outcomes do not reveal any significant variance between the environments of the two institutions.
